Mereological Theater
Prof. Laurent Pereira - SNU 2021
Project by Enrique Sanz-Gadea

This Performing Arts Center in Suwon explores fractality through the interplay between parts and wholes, enabling flexible configurations of space and performances.

Watch Presentation Film ►

Trisha Brown
Site Analysis
Building
Performances

This project delves into the creation of space from a mereological perspective by questioning the relationship between parts and wholes. The idea stems from a thorough analysis of the project site, where different objects were scattered around an old brick factory ruin. Using machine learning algorithms to examine the relationships between these objects, a hidden web of connections was uncovered that revealed how each element was linked to the others based on similarities and differences. These elements came together in countless ways to form different compositions that overlapped with each other, exploring the mereological universalist thesis that any assortment of stuff, no matter how scattered across time or space, can compose something. However, the program also demonstrated how some relations and compositions are more significant than others.

Following this concept, the different spaces within the project act as individual elements that have the possibility to join one another to compose a higher, more complex entity. The resulting shape tends towards fractality because smaller elements form bigger ones, which then join together to create even bigger elements. This brings the idea of emergence to life, where not only can two spaces join to create one larger and superior space, but two performances can also come together and complement each other to create something new, much like instruments in an orchestra. As such, the architecture's spatiality depends on the individual's perception, and the project leaves the door open for interpretation of what constitutes a limited space, and where one space ends and the next begins. From movable walls to topography, the project allows for various levels and scales of interpretation.

The project highlights the significance of ordinary objects, regaining their importance as seen in the brick factory site. To inhabit the spaces, the project uses easy-to-transport furniture, which allows for maximum flexibility in adapting to the program or type of performance. Specific areas have been designated for storing this material in both the inner and outer spaces. Moreover, a system of rails hanging from the ceiling enables heavier equipment required for performances to be carried around the architecture, despite its irregular topography.

The spaces are divided with a double partitioned sliding soundproof wall that works like a door, enabling spaces to be isolated from one another when required. The stages are built in a festival-like manner, and adjacent rooms can be used as a backstage or green room for performers or technicians, depending on the composition of the space at the moment. The whole project acts like a living organism that is ever-evolving, composed of numerous systems that enable it to adapt to society's cultural requirements, primarily due to its spatial flexibility.
